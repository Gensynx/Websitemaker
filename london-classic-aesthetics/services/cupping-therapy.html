Cupping Therapy

Dry and wet cupping (hijama), practised with modern hygiene standards and clinical care: an ancient therapy for tension, circulation and recovery.

IThe treatment

Cupping is one of the oldest therapies still in practice: cups placed on the skin create gentle suction, drawing blood flow to the area, easing muscular tension and leaving many clients with a distinct sense of release. Athletes rediscovered it; tradition never forgot it.

We offer both forms. Dry cupping uses suction alone and suits tension, tightness and recovery. Wet cupping, known as hijama, adds small, superficial incisions so a minimal amount of blood is drawn into the cup; it is a traditional practice we perform with full clinical hygiene: sterile, single-use cups and blades, gloved technique and proper aftercare.

What sets cupping at LCA apart is the setting. This is a doctor-led clinic, so screening is real, hygiene is uncompromising, and you are advised honestly on whether cupping suits your circumstances.

IIWho it suits, honestly

People come to cupping for different reasons: muscular, circulatory, traditional or simply the deep relaxation of it. It suits more people than most treatments, with sensible exceptions.

Consider it if

  • Neck, shoulder and back tension that massage only briefly touches
  • Heavy training loads and slow recovery
  • Wanting hijama performed to modern clinical hygiene standards
  • Feeling generally sluggish and wanting a traditional reset
  • Curiosity, honestly, and we welcome first-timers

It is not for

  • Blood disorders or anticoagulant medication, unless cleared first
  • Pregnancy, for wet cupping in particular
  • Skin infections or wounds at the intended sites
  • Severe anaemia or recent significant illness
  • Anyone uncomfortable with the temporary circular marks

IIIYour appointment, step by step

Whether dry or wet, the session is methodical and surprisingly restful.

  1. Consultation

    A short health screen and a conversation about what brings you in, which determines whether dry or wet cupping serves you better, and where the cups should go.

  2. Placement

    Skin is cleansed and cups are placed with controlled suction. The pull feels firm and unfamiliar for a minute, then most clients settle into deep relaxation.

  3. The therapy

    Dry cupping rests in place while circulation does the work. For hijama, cups are briefly lifted for small superficial incisions with sterile blades, then reapplied.

  4. Aftercare

    Sites are cleaned and dressed where needed, and you get written aftercare. The circular marks are normal, painless and fade over days.

Safety, before style. Single-use sterile cups and blades, gloved clinical technique, honest screening and clean dressing afterwards. Traditional therapy deserves modern standards, and here it gets them without exception.

IVQuestions, answered

Does cupping hurt?

The suction feels strong for the first minute, then fades into warmth and pressure most people find deeply relaxing. Hijama incisions are shallow and brief, closer to a scratch than a cut.

How long do the marks last?

The circular marks typically fade within several days to a week or so. They are drawn blood flow, not bruising from injury, and they do not hurt.

What is the difference between dry and wet cupping?

Dry cupping is suction only. Wet cupping (hijama) adds tiny superficial incisions so a small amount of blood is drawn into the cups, in keeping with the traditional practice, performed here with sterile single-use equipment.

How should I prepare?

Come lightly fed and hydrated rather than fasting hard, wear comfortable clothing, and plan an easy rest of the day. Full guidance comes with your booking.

How often should I have it?

It varies by goal and tradition. Some clients come at regular intervals, others seasonally. We will suggest a rhythm honestly rather than upsell frequency.
